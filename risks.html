Risks

The threats deepfakes pose to individuals, democracy, and society

The risks associated with deepfakes and AI-generated misinformation are wide-ranging and increasingly well-documented. They span individual harm — such as defamation and non-consensual intimate imagery — through to systemic threats to democratic processes and national security. As the technology becomes cheaper and more accessible, these risks are intensifying.

Severity note: Researchers have identified deepfakes as a Category 1 information hazard — one that combines mass reach, high believability, and low cost of production in ways that earlier forms of misinformation did not (Chesney & Citron, 2019).

Threats to Democracy and Political Processes

Election interference

Deepfake videos and synthetic audio can be used to fabricate statements by political candidates, spreading false information to voters at scale. The Taiwan 2024 presidential election case is one of the most documented examples: AI-generated audio clips falsely attributed inflammatory statements to candidates, spreading on TikTok and Facebook before fact-checkers could respond (Taiwan FactCheck Center, 2024). Even when debunked, these materials can leave lasting impressions — a phenomenon researchers call the "liar's dividend" (Chesney & Citron, 2019).

Erosion of public trust

Perhaps the most insidious long-term risk is not individual deepfakes, but the broader effect on public epistemology. When people know that any video or audio could be fabricated, genuine footage can be dismissed as fake. This creates a generalised distrust of authentic evidence — the so-called "liar's dividend" — that undermines journalism, legal proceedings, and democratic accountability (Vaccari & Chadwick, 2020).

Harms to Individuals

Non-consensual intimate imagery (NCII)

The most prevalent malicious use of deepfake technology is the creation of non-consensual pornographic imagery, with women — particularly public figures — being disproportionately targeted. Estimates suggest that over 90% of deepfake videos online are non-consensual intimate imagery (Ajder et al., 2019). The psychological, reputational, and professional harm to victims is severe and often long-lasting.

Defamation and reputational damage

Fabricated footage of individuals making offensive statements, committing crimes, or engaging in embarrassing behaviour can destroy careers and relationships. Unlike text-based defamation, deepfakes carry the intuitive authority of video evidence — making them far more damaging even after correction (Chesney & Citron, 2019).

Fraud and financial scams

Voice cloning has been used to impersonate corporate executives in audio calls, instructing employees to transfer funds — a form of "CEO fraud" that has cost organisations millions of dollars. Facial deepfakes have also been used to defeat biometric security systems (Kietzmann et al., 2021).

National Security and Geopolitical Risks

Military and diplomatic disinformation

Deepfake technology could be used by state actors to fabricate footage of military incidents, assassinations, or political statements to justify military action or destabilise foreign governments. The US Department of Defense has treated deepfakes as a national security threat, funding research into detection through the DARPA Media Forensics programme (Westerlund, 2019).

Challenges for Detection and Response

Detection is an arms race

Detection models trained on today's deepfakes become obsolete as generation technology improves. Research by Tolosana et al. (2020) found that state-of-the-art detectors regularly fail on deepfakes produced by newer generation models they were not trained on. This creates a permanent technological asymmetry where producers have an advantage over detectors.

Viral spread outpaces correction

Social media algorithms optimise for engagement. Emotionally provocative content — whether true or false — spreads faster than corrections. By the time a deepfake is debunked, it may have been seen by millions; the correction will typically reach far fewer people (Vaccari & Chadwick, 2020).
