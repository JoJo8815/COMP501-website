Risks

The threats deepfakes pose to individuals, democracy, and society

The risks associated with deepfakes and AI generated misinformation are wide-ranging and increasingly well-documented. They span from direct personal harms - non-consensual intimate imagery, defamation, fraud - through to systemic threats affecting democratic institutions and national security. What makes this threat landscape particularly difficult to address is the pace of change: risks that were theoretical five years ago are now documented real-world incidents (Chesney & Citron, 2019). As tools become cheaper, easier to use, and more widely available, the barriers that once limited deepfake misuse to well-resourced actors are disappearing.

A note on severity: Chesney and Citron (2019) classify deepfakes as a Category 1 information hazard - one that combines mass reach, high believability, and very low production cost in ways that earlier forms of misinformation could not. The concern is not just individual incidents, but the cumulative effect on public trust in media, evidence, and democratic institutions.

Threats to Democracy and Political Processes

Election interference

Deepfake videos and synthetic audio can fabricate statements by political candidates and spread them to voters at scale before any correction is possible. Taiwan's 2024 presidential election is the most thoroughly documented example: AI generated audio clips falsely attributed damaging statements to candidates and spread across TikTok and Facebook before fact-checkers could respond (Taiwan FactCheck Center, 2024). Research following the election found that even after the deepfakes were debunked, measurable increases in voter distrust and polarisation persisted - the correction simply did not reach as many people as the original fabrication (Hwang, 2024).

A deepfake released 48 hours before an election can cause real damage before any institutional response is possible. This is not a hypothetical - it is what happened in Taiwan, and there is no reason to think other democracies are immune.

Erosion of public trust - the "liar's dividend"

Perhaps the most insidious long-term risk is not any individual deepfake, but the broader effect on how people relate to media evidence. When convincing synthetic media becomes widely known to exist, genuine footage can simply be dismissed as fabricated. A politician caught on camera saying something damaging can claim the video is a deepfake - and in a media environment where fabrications are common, that claim becomes harder to refute. Chesney and Citron (2019) call this the "liar's dividend," and Vaccari and Chadwick (2020) found empirical evidence of it: exposure to deepfake content significantly increased participants' uncertainty about authentic political videos they had not previously doubted. This is a risk to the entire evidentiary foundation of democratic accountability.

Harms to Individuals

Non-consensual intimate imagery

The most prevalent malicious use of deepfake technology is the creation of non-consensual pornographic imagery. Women - and particularly women in public life - are disproportionately targeted. A 2019 report by Deeptrace Labs found that over 90% of deepfake videos online at that time were non-consensual intimate content (Ajder et al., 2019). It should be noted that this figure is now several years old; the absolute numbers have grown considerably since as tools became more accessible (Mirsky & Lee, 2021). The psychological, reputational, and professional harm to victims is severe and often long-lasting, compounded by how quickly content spreads before platforms can act.

In the New Zealand context, the Harmful Digital Communications Act 2015 provides some relevant coverage but was not designed with AI generated content in mind. It has significant gaps when it comes to attributing responsibility to anonymous creators or content originating overseas (Chesney & Citron, 2019).

Defamation and reputational damage

Fabricated footage of individuals making offensive statements or committing crimes can destroy careers and relationships. What distinguishes deepfakes from older forms of defamation is the intuitive authority of video - human beings are wired to trust what they see, and that instinct does not disappear just because we intellectually know deepfakes exist. Vaccari and Chadwick (2020) confirmed that deepfake video significantly increased viewer uncertainty even when participants were warned in advance that manipulation was possible. The damage persists long after corrections are issued, because corrections travel more slowly than the original content.

Fraud and financial crime

Voice cloning has been used to impersonate corporate executives in phone calls, instructing employees to transfer funds - a technique known as "CEO fraud" or "vishing." Several documented cases have resulted in losses of hundreds of thousands of dollars per incident (Kietzmann et al., 2021). Deepfakes have also been used to defeat biometric security systems such as facial recognition, which banks and border agencies increasingly rely on to verify identity.

National Security Risks

State-level disinformation

Deepfake technology in the hands of state actors could fabricate footage of military incidents or political statements designed to justify conflict, destabilise foreign governments, or inflame domestic populations. The US Department of Defense has treated this threat seriously enough to fund dedicated research into deepfake detection through the DARPA Media Forensics programme (Westerlund, 2019). The concern is not simply that false footage can be created - it is that the quality and accessibility of AI generated content makes it far harder for audiences, journalists, and courts to confidently distinguish it from authentic material.

Why These Risks Are Structurally Difficult to Address

Detection is a permanent arms race

Detection models are trained on known deepfakes and therefore always play catch-up with generation technology. Tolosana et al. (2020) found that state-of-the-art detectors regularly fail on deepfakes produced by generation models they were not trained on - which is to say, on anything new. Every improvement in detection incentivises a corresponding improvement in generation. This is not a temporary problem that better tooling will eventually solve; it is a structural asymmetry that may never close.

Viral spread consistently outpaces correction

Social media algorithms are optimised for engagement, not accuracy. Emotionally provocative content - true or false - spreads faster than corrections. By the time a deepfake is identified and debunked, it will typically have been seen by millions; the correction will reach far fewer (Vaccari & Chadwick, 2020). This is a structural feature of how attention works on social media platforms, not a temporary moderation failure. Addressing it requires changes to platform incentives, not just faster fact-checking.

Which Risks Matter Most?

Across the literature, two tiers of risk emerge. The first - non-consensual intimate imagery and targeted defamation - involves direct, immediate harm to identifiable individuals and demands clear legal responses. The second - election interference and the erosion of epistemic trust - involves diffuse, systemic harm to democratic institutions. These are harder to legislate but arguably more consequential in the long run, because they undermine the shared informational environment that democratic decision-making depends on. Any serious response needs to address both tiers, recognising that they require different tools and involve different actors.
