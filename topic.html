Understanding Deepfakes and AI-Generated Misinformation

What is a Deepfake?

The word deepfake combines two things: deep learning - a branch of artificial intelligence - and the word fake. A deepfake is any video, image, or audio clip that has been created or substantially altered using AI to make a real person appear to say or do something they never actually did (Chesney & Citron, 2019). Unlike older photo-editing techniques, deepfakes use machine learning models trained on large datasets of real footage, which is what makes them qualitatively different from anything that came before.

What makes this technology particularly alarming is how convincing it has become in a very short time. Early face-swap tools produced obvious artefacts - blurry edges, unnatural blinking, mismatched lighting. Modern deepfakes can replicate a person's voice, facial expressions, and micro-movements with remarkable accuracy, to the point where trained experts and automated detection systems regularly fail to identify them (Tolosana et al., 2020). The speed of this improvement has outpaced both public awareness and the legal frameworks designed to address it.

Key definition: A deepfake is synthetic media generated by artificial intelligence to fabricate realistic depictions of real people saying or doing things that never occurred. The term first appeared on Reddit in 2017 and has since entered mainstream vocabulary as the technology spread rapidly beyond research settings (Chesney & Citron, 2019).

How Are Deepfakes Made?

The primary technology behind deepfakes is the Generative Adversarial Network (GAN), first introduced by Goodfellow et al. (2014). A GAN works by setting two competing neural networks against each other in a continuous feedback loop:

THE GENERATOR
Produces synthetic images or video frames, trying to create output realistic enough to fool the discriminator. Every time it fails, it adjusts and tries again - progressively getting better at producing convincing fakes.
THE DISCRIMINATOR
Acts as a critic, comparing generated content against real footage and identifying what looks fake. As it improves at detection, it forces the generator to produce increasingly convincing results in return.

Through thousands of training iterations, this adversarial process produces synthetic media of escalating realism. Neither network is programmed with rules about what a human face looks like - they learn entirely from data, which is what makes the output so difficult to detect (Goodfellow et al., 2014). More recent architectures, including diffusion models and transformer-based approaches, have further improved output quality while reducing the computing power required, bringing the technology within reach of non-experts (Kietzmann et al., 2021).

Types of Synthetic Media

Face-swap deepfakes

The most common type. A target person's face is mapped onto another individual's body in existing video footage. Early versions required hours of training footage and significant computing resources; modern tools can produce convincing results from as little as a single photograph. This accessibility is what has driven the rapid increase in malicious use (Mirsky & Lee, 2021).

Voice cloning (audio deepfakes)

AI systems trained on a person's voice recordings can generate entirely new speech, making that person appear to say anything. Commercially available tools can now clone a voice from as little as thirty seconds of sample audio. This technique was central to the disinformation campaign observed during Taiwan's 2024 presidential election (Taiwan FactCheck Center, 2024).

Text-to-video synthesis

Emerging models can generate entirely fabricated video from a text prompt, without requiring any source footage of the target individual. This is a significant escalation - whereas earlier deepfakes needed real footage to manipulate, these tools can invent scenarios from scratch. The implications for evidence, journalism, and public trust are not yet fully understood (Westerlund, 2019).

Cheapfakes

Not all manipulated media requires deep learning. Cheapfakes use simpler techniques - slowed or sped-up footage, misleading captions, or clips taken deliberately out of context. Paris and Donovan (2019) argue these are often more dangerous in practice than technically sophisticated deepfakes, because they are faster to produce and require no specialist knowledge whatsoever.

Case Study: Taiwan's 2024 Presidential Election

Taiwan's January 2024 presidential election is one of the most thoroughly documented cases of AI generated misinformation being used in a real political context. Researchers at the Taiwan FactCheck Center identified multiple AI generated audio clips falsely attributing inflammatory statements to candidates, as well as manipulated video footage, all of which spread across TikTok, Facebook, and X within hours of being posted (Taiwan FactCheck Center, 2024).

The deepfakes spread to millions of viewers before fact-checkers could issue corrections. While no definitive evidence emerged that the outcome of the election was changed, researchers measured meaningful increases in voter distrust and political polarisation following each viral deepfake incident (Hwang, 2024). This points to the "liar's dividend" described by Chesney and Citron (2019) - the cumulative effect of repeated exposure to synthetic political media eroding trust in all media, genuine or not. Taiwan also offered useful lessons about preparation: the Taiwan FactCheck Center had established fact-checking infrastructure specifically in anticipation of AI generated content, and their experience shows how much faster misinformation moves even when systems are in place.

How the Technology Has Developed

The term "deepfake" first appeared on Reddit in late 2017, when a user shared face-swapped videos created using open-source machine learning code. Within two years, the technology had become accessible enough that non-experts could produce convincing results using free mobile applications (Chesney & Citron, 2019). A 2019 report by Deeptrace Labs found the number of deepfake videos online had nearly doubled in nine months, with non-consensual pornographic content making up the vast majority (Ajder et al., 2019). By 2024, commercial tools capable of voice cloning and video synthesis were widely available, requiring nothing more than a short sample of audio or video as input.

The key takeaway from this trajectory is that the deepfake problem is not static. Each year the technology becomes more capable, cheaper, and easier to use - while detection methods, legal frameworks, and public awareness consistently lag behind. Understanding that gap is the central challenge this project aims to address.
