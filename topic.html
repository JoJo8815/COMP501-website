Understanding Deepfakes and AI-Generated Misinformation

What is a Deepfake?

The word deepfake is a combination of two terms: deep learning — a branch of artificial intelligence — and fake. A deepfake is any video, image, or audio clip that has been created or manipulated using AI to make a real person appear to say or do something they never actually did (Chesney & Citron, 2019). Unlike older photo-editing techniques, deepfakes use machine learning models trained on large datasets of real footage to produce synthetic media that can be nearly indistinguishable from authentic recordings.

What makes deepfakes particularly alarming is how convincing they have become. Early face-swap technology produced obvious visual artefacts. Modern deepfakes can replicate a person's voice, facial expressions, and micro-movements with remarkable accuracy — to the point where trained experts and automated detection tools can struggle to identify them (Tolosana et al., 2020).

Key definition: A deepfake is synthetic media generated by artificial intelligence, specifically deep learning algorithms, to fabricate realistic depictions of real individuals saying or doing things that did not occur.

How Are Deepfakes Made?

The primary technology behind deepfakes is the Generative Adversarial Network (GAN), first introduced by Goodfellow et al. (2014). A GAN consists of two competing neural networks:

THE GENERATOR
Produces synthetic images or video frames, attempting to create output realistic enough to fool the discriminator. It learns from each failed attempt, progressively improving its output.
THE DISCRIMINATOR
Acts as a critic, comparing generated content against real footage and identifying fakes. As it improves at detection, it forces the generator to produce increasingly convincing results.

Through thousands of training iterations, this adversarial process produces synthetic media of escalating realism. More recent architectures, such as diffusion models and transformer-based approaches, have further improved quality and reduced the computational cost of generation (Kietzmann et al., 2021).

Types of Synthetic Media

Face-swap deepfakes

The most common type. A target person's face is mapped onto another individual's body in video footage. Early examples were produced manually; modern tools can do this automatically with minutes of training footage.

Voice cloning (audio deepfakes)

AI systems trained on recordings of a person's voice can generate new speech — making that person appear to say anything. This technique was used in the Taiwan 2024 election incident.

Text-to-video synthesis

Emerging models can generate entirely fabricated video from text prompts, without requiring any source footage of the target individual.

Cheapfakes

Lower-tech manipulations — slowed footage, misleading captions, spliced clips — that do not use deep learning but are often grouped with deepfakes due to similar intent and impact (Paris & Donovan, 2019).

Case Study: Taiwan's 2024 Presidential Election

Taiwan's January 2024 presidential election provided one of the most documented examples of deepfakes being deployed in a real political context. Researchers at the Taiwan FactCheck Center identified AI-generated audio clips purportedly featuring candidates making inflammatory or damaging statements, as well as manipulated video footage shared across TikTok, Facebook, and X (formerly Twitter) (Taiwan FactCheck Center, 2024).

These synthetic media pieces spread rapidly — often within hours of being posted — before fact-checking organisations could issue corrections. While researchers concluded that the deepfakes did not determine the election outcome, studies found measurable increases in voter distrust and political polarisation in the periods following viral deepfake circulation (Hwang, 2024).

Historical Development

The term "deepfake" first appeared on Reddit in 2017, when a user shared face-swapped celebrity videos created using open-source machine learning code. Within two years, the technology had become accessible enough that non-experts could produce convincing results using freely available applications (Chesney & Citron, 2019). By 2024, commercial AI tools capable of voice cloning and video synthesis were widely available, many requiring only a short audio or video sample as input.
