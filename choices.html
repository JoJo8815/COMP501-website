Choices

How society can respond - technology, policy, and individual action

No single intervention is sufficient to address the deepfake problem. Technical detection tools can be evaded as generation technology improves. Laws are slow to pass and difficult to enforce across borders. Platform moderation policies exist on paper but are applied inconsistently. A serious response requires coordinated action across multiple levels - technical, legal, platform-level, and educational - with realistic expectations about what each layer can and cannot achieve on its own.

The central ethical question: When a deepfake causes harm, who is responsible? The AI company that built the tool? The platform that let it spread? The individual who created it? The government that failed to legislate in time? Our research suggests responsibility is genuinely shared across all four - but it is not shared equally, and the current environment places too much of the burden on individuals while the most powerful actors remain largely unaccountable.

Technical Responses

Deepfake detection tools

Machine learning classifiers trained on known deepfakes can flag suspicious content for human review. Microsoft's Video Authenticator and Meta's Deepfake Detection Challenge represent significant investment in this area. However, the core limitation is well-established in the literature: detection accuracy degrades rapidly on content produced by generation models the classifier has not been trained on (Tolosana et al., 2020). As Mirsky and Lee (2021) note, the relationship between detection and generation is inherently adversarial - every improvement in detection becomes a target for generation researchers to overcome. Detection tools are worth investing in, but they should be understood as one layer of a broader strategy, not a standalone solution.

Content provenance and digital watermarking

The Coalition for Content Provenance and Authenticity (C2PA) is developing open standards for cryptographically signing media at the point of creation, so that cameras and editing tools attach a verifiable record of how content was produced and modified (C2PA, 2023). This is structurally more promising than detection alone because it shifts the burden of proof - rather than trying to identify fakes after the fact, it creates a system where authentic content can be positively verified. The significant limitation is adoption: the system only works if it becomes universal, and there is currently no mechanism to compel that (Kietzmann et al., 2021).

AI watermarking of generated content

Several AI companies have begun embedding invisible watermarks into content their models produce. Google DeepMind's SynthID embeds watermarks in AI generated images and audio designed to survive common editing and compression. If universally adopted, this could enable automatic identification of synthetic media at scale. The practical challenge is that the actors most likely to misuse deepfake tools are the least likely to use platforms that watermark their output.

Platform and Industry Responses

Platform content moderation

Meta, YouTube, and TikTok have all introduced policies prohibiting malicious deepfakes, particularly non-consensual intimate imagery and political manipulation. The existence of these policies is a step forward, but enforcement is the problem: Vaccari and Chadwick (2020) found that viral deepfake content typically accumulated millions of views before any platform action was taken, by which point the damage was done. Reactive moderation has inherent structural limits. Enforcement is also consistently weaker in non-English-language contexts where platforms have fewer content moderation resources.

Mandatory disclosure requirements

Several jurisdictions have begun requiring that AI generated content in political contexts be clearly labelled. California's 2024 legislation requiring disclosure of deepfakes in political advertising within 90 days of an election is one example. Extending such requirements globally is challenging because social media content crosses borders instantly while legislation moves at the pace of individual national parliaments (Hwang, 2024). Disclosure requirements also only address the most visible harms and do nothing about deepfakes that are not presented as political advertising.

Legal and Regulatory Responses

Targeted legislation for specific harms

The most effective legislative responses have targeted specific, well-defined harms rather than attempting to regulate deepfakes as a broad category. The UK's Online Safety Act 2023 criminalised the sharing of non-consensual intimate deepfakes - a focused intervention addressing a clearly documented harm. South Korea and China have introduced specific deepfake legislation covering both intimate imagery and electoral interference. These targeted approaches are more achievable politically and more legally defensible than broad bans on synthetic media (Chesney & Citron, 2019).

New Zealand's regulatory position

In New Zealand, the Harmful Digital Communications Act 2015 provides some coverage for deepfake harms - particularly non-consensual intimate imagery - but it was drafted before AI generated content became a practical reality. The Act's focus on communications between identifiable individuals creates gaps when it comes to algorithmically amplified synthetic media, anonymous creators, and content originating overseas. New Zealand has not yet introduced deepfake-specific legislation, leaving a meaningful gap compared to the UK, South Korea, and several other jurisdictions. Given the pace at which the technology is developing, proactive reform is far more likely to be effective than waiting to respond to specific incidents.

International coordination

Because deepfakes can be created in one country and spread globally within minutes, effective regulation ultimately requires international cooperation. The EU AI Act includes provisions on synthetic media and biometric manipulation that set a meaningful standard, though their enforcement outside EU jurisdiction remains limited. Achieving consistent global standards presents real diplomatic challenges, but the alternative - a patchwork of national laws with no extraterritorial reach - leaves most of the problem unaddressed (Hwang, 2024).

Education and Individual Responses

Digital media literacy

Educating the public to be more critical consumers of digital media is one of the most sustainable long-term responses available, even if it is the slowest. Recognising signs of manipulation - unnatural blinking, lighting inconsistencies, audio-visual sync errors - and developing habits of source verification before sharing can reduce the impact of deepfakes even before technical and legal solutions are in place (Westerlund, 2019). However, media literacy alone is not sufficient. Vaccari and Chadwick (2020) found that even participants instructed to be sceptical still showed measurable increases in uncertainty about authentic media after exposure to deepfakes. Individual scepticism is a meaningful complement to structural solutions, not a substitute for them.

Our Assessment: What Actually Works

Based on the evidence across the literature, no single intervention is sufficient, but some are structurally more sound than others. Detection tools will always lag behind generation and should not be treated as a primary defence. Content provenance standards like C2PA are more promising in principle but require near-universal adoption to be effective. Targeted legislation addressing specific harms has proven more achievable and enforceable than broad regulatory approaches.

Drawing on Chesney and Citron (2019) and Kietzmann et al. (2021), we argue the most defensible response combines: technical watermarking standards adopted by AI developers as a baseline requirement; consistent platform enforcement with meaningful consequences rather than voluntary commitments; targeted legislation covering non-consensual intimate imagery and electoral deepfakes specifically; and sustained investment in digital media literacy, particularly in schools. Critically, responsibility should not fall primarily on individuals to detect and resist synthetic misinformation. The companies that build and distribute these tools at scale have both the capacity and the obligation to do more - and the regulatory environment should reflect that.
