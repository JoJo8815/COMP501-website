Choices

How society can respond — technology, policy, and individual action

Addressing the deepfake problem requires action at multiple levels simultaneously. No single intervention is sufficient: technical detection tools can be evaded; laws are slow to pass and difficult to enforce across borders; platform policies are applied inconsistently. A comprehensive response requires coordinated choices by AI developers, social media platforms, governments, and individuals.

Central ethical question: When a deepfake causes harm — who is responsible? The AI company that built the tool? The platform that allowed it to spread? The individual who created it? Or governments that failed to legislate in time? Our research suggests responsibility is shared, but unevenly borne.

Technical Responses

Deepfake detection tools

Machine learning classifiers trained on known deepfakes can flag suspicious content for human review. Tools such as Microsoft's Video Authenticator and Meta's deepfake detection challenge entries represent significant investment in this area. However, Tolosana et al. (2020) note that detection accuracy degrades rapidly on content produced by generation models the classifier was not trained on — making this an ongoing arms race rather than a solved problem.

Content provenance and digital watermarking

The Coalition for Content Provenance and Authenticity (C2PA) is developing open standards for cryptographically signing media at the point of creation, so that cameras and editing tools can attach a verifiable record of how content was produced and modified. This approach — sometimes called "nutrition labels for media" — would allow users to verify whether content is authentic or AI-generated, without relying on detection algorithms (Kietzmann et al., 2021).

AI watermarking of generated content

Several AI companies have begun embedding invisible watermarks in content produced by their models. Google DeepMind's SynthID embeds watermarks in AI-generated images and audio that survive editing and compression. If adopted universally, this could enable automatic identification of synthetic media at scale.

Platform and Industry Responses

Platform content policies

Major platforms including Meta, YouTube, and TikTok have introduced policies prohibiting malicious deepfakes — particularly non-consensual intimate imagery and political manipulation. However, enforcement is inconsistent and often reactive. Vaccari and Chadwick (2020) found that viral deepfake content typically accumulates millions of views before platform action is taken, by which point the damage is done.

Mandatory disclosure requirements

Some jurisdictions are requiring that AI-generated content in political advertising be clearly labelled. The US state of California passed legislation in 2024 requiring disclosure of deepfakes in political advertisements within 90 days of an election. Extending such requirements globally remains a significant challenge given the cross-border nature of social media platforms.

Legal and Regulatory Responses

Criminalisation of malicious deepfakes

Several countries have introduced or proposed specific legislation targeting non-consensual deepfake intimate imagery and election interference. The UK's Online Safety Act 2023 criminalised sharing non-consensual intimate deepfakes. South Korea and China have introduced specific deepfake legislation. New Zealand's Harmful Digital Communications Act provides some coverage, though was not designed with deepfakes specifically in mind (Chesney & Citron, 2019).

International coordination

Because deepfakes can be produced in one country and spread globally within hours, effective regulation requires international coordination. The EU AI Act includes provisions targeting synthetic media and biometric manipulation. Achieving consistent global standards remains an open policy challenge (Hwang, 2024).

Education and Individual Responses

Digital media literacy

Educating the public to be critical consumers of digital media is one of the most sustainable long-term responses. Recognising the signs of manipulation — unnatural blinking, lighting inconsistencies, audio-visual sync errors — and developing habits of source verification can reduce the impact of deepfakes even before technical and legal solutions are in place (Westerlund, 2019).

Our group's recommendation

Based on our research, we conclude that no single approach is sufficient. The most effective response combines: (1) technical watermarking standards adopted by AI developers; (2) consistent platform enforcement with meaningful consequences; (3) targeted legislation that addresses non-consensual imagery and political manipulation specifically; and (4) investment in digital media literacy education, particularly in schools and communities with high social media exposure. Responsibility should not fall on individuals alone — the companies that build and distribute these tools must be held accountable.
