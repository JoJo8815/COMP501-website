Opportunities

Legitimate and beneficial uses of synthetic media technology

Most public discussion of deepfakes focuses almost entirely on their potential for harm, which is understandable given the documented examples of misuse. But the underlying technologies - Generative Adversarial Networks, voice synthesis, and video generation - also have genuine, well-documented beneficial applications. Understanding these matters for two reasons: it produces a more honest picture of the technology, and any policy response that ignores legitimate uses risks restricting tools that deliver real social value alongside the harm (Kietzmann et al., 2021). The question is not whether these opportunities exist, but whether society can build frameworks that protect them while preventing abuse.

An important condition: Every legitimate application on this page depends on two things - informed consent from any person whose likeness or voice is used, and clear disclosure to audiences that the content is AI generated. Without those conditions, even well-intentioned applications become ethically problematic. This is not a minor caveat; it is the line that separates legitimate synthetic media from deepfake abuse.

Film, Television, and Creative Industries

Digital de-ageing and posthumous performance

Film studios have used deepfake-adjacent technology to de-age actors for flashback sequences or to complete performances from actors who passed away mid-production. Disney's use of synthetic media in productions such as The Mandalorian demonstrated that these tools can serve legitimate storytelling purposes when used with appropriate consent and transparent disclosure (Kietzmann et al., 2021). That said, posthumous digital performances raise unresolved ethical questions - particularly around whether consent given in advance can meaningfully cover all future uses, and who holds the right to authorise them after the actor has died.

Localisation and dubbing

AI-powered lip-sync technology can match dubbed dialogue to an actor's original mouth movements, improving the viewing experience for international audiences. Traditional dubbing has always involved a disconnect between what viewers see and hear; synthetic media offers a way to close that gap and expand access to global content for hundreds of millions of people (Westerlund, 2019). The critical ethical requirement here is actor consent - using someone's likeness without agreement, even for benign purposes, crosses a clear line.

Education and Training

Historical recreation

Synthetic media can bring historical figures to life for educational purposes, allowing students to engage with realistic simulations of past events. Museums and documentary makers have explored AI generated representations to enhance accessibility and engagement. The critical requirement - one not always met in practice - is clear labelling. When AI recreations are presented without explicit disclosure, they stop being educational tools and start distorting the historical record (Paris & Donovan, 2019).

Language learning and personalised tutoring

Voice synthesis can produce personalised language-learning content in dozens of languages at a fraction of the cost of recording human teachers. Combined with interactive AI, this opens up scalable education in under-resourced communities where qualified language teachers are scarce (Westerlund, 2019). This is one of the clearest cases where the technology's benefits are difficult to replicate by any other means.

Accessibility

Assistive technology for people with speech impairments

Voice cloning can preserve a person's natural voice before they lose it to conditions such as ALS or throat cancer, allowing them to continue communicating in their own voice through text-to-speech systems for the rest of their life (Chesney & Citron, 2019). This is perhaps the most ethically straightforward application of the technology - a person consenting to clone their own voice, for their own future use, causes no harm to anyone. It is a reminder that the ethical problems with deepfakes are almost always about the absence of consent, not the technology itself.

Sign language avatars

AI generated avatars capable of rendering sign language from spoken or written input could make digital content far more accessible for Deaf communities, reducing dependence on human interpreters for on-demand content such as news broadcasts, public health announcements, and educational materials.

Healthcare and Research

Medical training simulations

Synthetic video of patient consultations and clinical procedures can supplement medical training without the ethical and logistical challenges of using real patient footage. GANs have also been used to generate synthetic medical imaging data - X-rays, MRI scans - to train diagnostic AI while protecting patient privacy (Tolosana et al., 2020). Training data for rare conditions is hard to obtain, and synthetic generation offers a practical way to build robust datasets without compromising confidentiality.

Detection and Counter-Research

Advancing deepfake detection

Building effective detection systems requires access to large quantities of deepfake content to train on. Researchers use GANs to generate synthetic media datasets, which are then used to train the classifiers that identify forgeries (Tolosana et al., 2020). Without this research application, detection technology would lag even further behind generation. This is worth keeping in mind when considering how to regulate deepfake tools - blanket restrictions that prevent research use could inadvertently hamper the development of the detection systems needed to address the problem.

Weighing the Opportunities Against the Risks

Taken together, these applications show that synthetic media technology is not inherently harmful. In several cases - voice banking for people with terminal illness, language learning in under-resourced communities, synthetic medical imaging - the benefits are real and difficult to achieve by other means. The problem is not the existence of the technology; it is the absence of consistent consent frameworks, disclosure requirements, and legal protections. Kietzmann et al. (2021) make this point directly: the goal of policy should be to build a regulatory environment in which legitimate uses are protected and abusive uses are clearly prohibited - not to eliminate synthetic media altogether. That distinction shapes everything discussed in the Risks and Choices sections that follow.
