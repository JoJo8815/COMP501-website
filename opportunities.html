Opportunities

Legitimate and beneficial uses of synthetic media technology

While deepfakes are predominantly discussed in the context of harm, the underlying technologies — Generative Adversarial Networks, voice synthesis, and video generation — have significant legitimate applications. Understanding these opportunities is important for developing proportionate and balanced policy responses that do not eliminate beneficial uses while addressing malicious ones.

Important distinction: The opportunities described here apply to synthetic media technology in general. All ethical applications require informed consent from any individuals whose likeness or voice is used, and clear disclosure to audiences that content is AI-generated.

Film, Television, and Creative Industries

Digital de-ageing and posthumous performance

Film studios have used deepfake-adjacent technology to de-age actors for flashback sequences or to complete performances by actors who passed away during production. Disney's use of synthetic media in productions such as The Mandalorian demonstrated how these tools can serve legitimate storytelling purposes when used with appropriate consent and disclosure (Kietzmann et al., 2021).

Localisation and dubbing

AI-powered lip-sync technology can match dubbed dialogue to an actor's original mouth movements, dramatically improving the viewing experience for international audiences. Companies such as Flawless AI are developing tools that could make foreign-language dubbing nearly seamless, expanding access to global media content (Vaccari & Chadwick, 2020).

Education and Training

Historical recreation

Synthetic media can bring historical figures to life for educational purposes, allowing students to interact with realistic simulations of historical events. Museums and documentary makers have explored AI-generated representations of historical figures to enhance engagement — provided these are clearly labelled as reconstructions.

Language learning and personalised tutoring

Voice synthesis can create personalised language-learning content delivered in a consistent, natural voice across many languages, reducing the cost of producing educational audio material. Combined with interactive AI, this opens opportunities for accessible, scalable education in under-resourced communities (Westerlund, 2019).

Accessibility

Assistive technologies for people with speech impairments

Voice cloning technology can preserve a person's voice before they lose it to conditions such as ALS or throat cancer, allowing them to continue communicating in their own voice via text-to-speech systems. Projects such as the "Voice Banking" initiative exemplify the profound human benefit this technology can deliver when applied ethically (Chesney & Citron, 2019).

Sign language avatars

AI-generated avatars capable of rendering sign language from text or spoken input could make digital content far more accessible to Deaf communities, reducing dependence on human interpreters for on-demand content.

Healthcare and Research

Medical training simulations

Synthetic video of patient consultations, clinical procedures, or mental health scenarios can supplement medical and psychology training without the ethical and logistical challenges of using real patient footage. GANs have also been used to generate synthetic medical imaging data to train diagnostic AI while preserving patient privacy (Tolosana et al., 2020).

Detection and Counter-Research

Advancing deepfake detection

Paradoxically, deepfake generation technology is essential to building better detection systems. Researchers use GANs to create large datasets of synthetic media, which are then used to train classifiers capable of identifying forgeries. Without access to generation tools, detection research would be severely limited (Tolosana et al., 2020).
